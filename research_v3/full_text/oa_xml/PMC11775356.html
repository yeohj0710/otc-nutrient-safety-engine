Oral Versus Injectable Vitamin D Therapy for Treating Nutritional Rickets in Indian Children: A Comparative Study

Abstract

Background

Rickets is a common metabolic bone disease in children, primarily caused by vitamin D deficiency. This study aimed to compare the efficacy of oral weekly vitamin D supplementation and injectable stoss therapy in treating nutritional rickets in Indian children.

Methods

This prospective, randomized, controlled trial was conducted over 18 months at a tertiary care center. Forty children aged 6 months to 16 years with clinical, biochemical, and radiological evidence of nutritional rickets were enrolled and randomly assigned to receive either oral vitamin D3 (60,000 IU weekly for 10 weeks) or a single intramuscular injection of vitamin D3 (600,000 IU). Clinical, biochemical, and radiological assessments were conducted at baseline and at 3 weeks, 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months post-treatment.

Results

Both treatment regimens significantly improved vitamin D, calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, and parathyroid hormone (PTH) levels, with no significant differences between the groups. Radiological healing, assessed by Thacher’s score, was achieved in both groups by 6 months. While both treatments were effective, injectable stoss therapy resulted in a more sustained increase in vitamin D levels and may offer better compliance due to its single-dose administration. No cases of local skin complications or vitamin D toxicity or symptomatic hypercalcemia were observed.

Conclusion

Oral weekly and injectable stoss therapies are both effective and safe for treating nutritional rickets. Injectable stoss therapy may be more suitable for the Indian population due to its cost-effectiveness and lower compliance demands. Serum parathyroid hormone (PTH) levels emerged as a useful early marker of rickets severity as well as treatment response. Early diagnosis and treatment are crucial to prevent long-term skeletal deformities.

Keywords: Nutritional, Rickets, Vitamin D, Deficiency, Stoss, Therapy

Introduction

Rickets, derived from the English word “wricken” meaning “to bend,” is a disease affecting growing children due to the failure of mineralization of the growth plate and osteoid matrix [1]. It is caused by defects in chondrocyte differentiation, mineralization of the epiphyseal growth plate, and osteoid mineralization. Rickets is one of the most prevalent metabolic bone diseases, primarily due to deficiencies in vitamin D, calcium, or phosphorus. Although it is endemic in developing tropical countries, there has been a re-emergence of rickets in developed countries due to inadequate sunlight exposure and prolonged breastfeeding [2].

In India, despite ample sunlight exposure, vitamin D deficiency is common across all age groups and sexes [3]. Factors contributing to this deficiency include low dietary calcium, skin colour, and changing lifestyles. Vitamin D deficiency is particularly prevalent in infancy due to decreased dietary intake, cultural practices, exclusive breastfeeding, and low maternal vitamin D levels. The highest incidence of rickets is observed in infants, young children aged 6–23 months, and adolescents aged 12–15 years [4].

Nutritional rickets, a type of calcipenic rickets, is primarily caused by vitamin D insufficiency [5]. Vitamin D is essential for skeletal health as it facilitates the absorption of calcium and phosphorus, promoting bone mineralization, development, and remodelling. The various causes of vitamin D deficiency include inadequate sunlight exposure, nutritional deficiency, maternal vitamin D deficiency, prematurity, exclusive breastfeeding, obesity, extensive burn injuries, chronic renal disease, gastrointestinal disease or bypass, liver disease, and certain medications [2].

Common recommendations for treating rickets include vitamin D supplementation through oral or injectable forms. The total dose of vitamin D is more predictive of the outcome than the frequency of dosing [6]. This study aims to compare the efficiency of oral and injectable vitamin D supplementation in treating nutritional rickets in children. The subjects received either oral or injectable vitamin D and were followed up at regular intervals for up to six months.

Materials and Methods

This study was designed as a prospective, randomized, controlled trial conducted over a period of eighteen months at a tertiary care centre.

Patient Selection Criteria

  • Inclusion Criteria
    • o
      Children aged 6 months to 16 years.
    • o
      Clinical, biochemical, and radiological evidence of nutritional rickets.
    • o
      Parents/guardians provided informed consent for participation in the study.
  • Exclusion Criteria
    • o
      Children with other forms of rickets (e.g., renal, genetic).
    • o
      Presence of underlying conditions affecting bone metabolism (e.g., chronic kidney disease, liver disease).
    • o
      Previous treatment with high-dose vitamin D in the past six months.
    • o
      Severe malnutrition or other systemic illnesses.

Assessment

  • History Taking:
    • o
      Detailed dietary history focusing on vitamin D and calcium intake.
    • o
      History of sunlight exposure.
    • o
      Previous medical history and any medications taken.
  • Physical Examination:
    • o
      Assessment for clinical signs of rickets such as bowed legs, wrist widening, and delayed milestones.
    • o
      Measurement of growth parameters including weight, height/length, and head circumference.
  • Biochemical Tests:
    • o
      Blood samples were collected for the following tests:
    • o
      Serum calcium.
    • o
      Serum phosphorus.
    • o
      Alkaline phosphatase.
    • o
      Serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] levels.
    • o
      Parathyroid hormone (PTH) levels.

  • Radiological Examination:
    • o
      X-rays of the wrists and knees to detect signs of rickets such as metaphyseal widening, cupping, and fraying—scoring done according to 10-point scoring system by Thacher et al.[20]

Intervention

  • Subjects diagnosed with nutritional rickets were randomly assigned to one of two treatment groups:

  • Oral Group:
    • o
      Subjects in this group received oral vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) at a dose of 60,000 IU weekly for 10 weeks.
  • Injectable Group:
    • o
      Subjects in this group received a single intramuscular injection of 600,000 IU of vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol).
  • Children in both the groups were given the following in common-
    • o
      Calcium-50 mg/kg/day
    • o
      Multivitamin and multimineral syrup- 2 tablespoons/day
    • o
      Advice for adequate sunlight exposure if required
    • o
      Advice for dietary modifications if required

Follow-Up Schedule and Assessment

  • Subjects were followed up at 3 weeks, 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months post-treatment. During each follow-up visit, the following assessments were performed:

  • Clinical Assessment:
    • o
      Evaluation of improvement in clinical symptoms such as bone pain, muscle weakness, and growth parameters.
    • o
      Physical examination to check for resolution of rickets signs.
  • Biochemical Tests:
    • o
      Repeat blood tests to measure serum calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, parathyroid hormone (PTH) and 25(OH)D levels.
  • Radiological Examination:
    • o
      Follow-up X-rays of the wrists and knees to assess the healing of rickets lesions and calculate Thacher’s score.

Data Collection and Analysis

  • Data were collected at baseline and during each follow-up visit.

  • Clinical data were recorded in standardized forms.

  • Biochemical and radiological data were entered into a secure database.

Results

Statistical Analysis

  • Descriptive statistics were used to summarize the baseline characteristics of the subjects.

  • Paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests were used to compare pre- and post-treatment values within each group.

  • Independent t tests or Mann–Whitney U tests were used to compare the outcomes between the two groups.

  • A p value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

  • Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS PC 21.

Study Population and Demographics

  • The study included 40 children with nutritional rickets, divided into two groups—an oral weekly regimen group (n = 20) and an injectable stoss regimen group (n = 20). Key observations included:

  • Age: Majority were in the age group of up to 5 years in both the oral weekly regimen group (65%) and the injectable stoss regimen group (60%), followed by the 6–10 years age group (20%).

  • Mean age of study subjects—Oral group: 4.71 ± 3.74, Injectable group: 5.80 ± 4.19 (p = 0.41)

  • Gender distribution—Oral group – 8 males, 12 females; Injectable group—10 males, 10 females. A higher proportion of females were in the oral weekly regimen group (60%) compared to the stoss regimen group (50%), though the difference was not statistically significant (p = 0.52).

Biochemical Parameters

  • Vitamin D Levels: No significant differences were observed between the regimens at any time point. Both regimens significantly increased vitamin D levels from baseline at all subsequent time points, with notable increases at 6 weeks compared to 3 weeks, and 3 months compared to 3 weeks for the oral regimen. [Fig. 1]

  • Serum Calcium Levels: No significant differences were observed between regimens until 6 months, where the oral regimen had significantly higher levels (p = 0.03). Both regimens significantly increased calcium levels from baseline, with notable increases at 6 weeks and 3 months compared to 3 weeks. The oral regimen showed a significant increase at 6 months compared to 3 weeks.

  • Serum Phosphorus Levels: No significant differences were observed between regimens. Both regimens significantly increased phosphorus levels from baseline. The injectable regimen showed significant increases at 6 weeks compared to 3 weeks, and at 6 months compared to 6 weeks.

  • Alkaline Phosphatase Levels: The injectable regimen had significantly higher levels at the 1st visit and 6 weeks. Both regimens showed significant reductions in levels from baseline, with notable decreases at 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months compared to 3 weeks. [Fig. 2]

  • Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) Levels: The injectable regimen had significantly higher levels at the 1st visit. Both regimens significantly reduced PTH levels from baseline, with significant decreases at 6 weeks and 3 months compared to 3 weeks. The injectable regimen also showed significant reductions at 6 months compared to 3 weeks. [Fig. 3]

Overall, both regimens were effective in improving vitamin D, calcium, phosphorus, alkaline phosphatase, and PTH levels, with some differences in the timing and extent of changes between the two regimens.

Radiological Parameters

There was no significant difference in Thacher’s score between the oral weekly and injectable stoss regimens at the 1st visit, 3 weeks, 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months (p > 0.05). Both regimens showed significantly lower Thacher’s scores at 3 weeks, 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months compared to baseline (p < 0.05). Significant reductions in Thacher’s score were observed at 6 weeks, 3 months, and 6 months compared to 3 weeks, as well as between 3 months and 6 weeks, and between 6 months and 6 weeks in both regimens (p < 0.001).

Clinical Parameters

  • Participants on the oral weekly regimen showed improvements in several conditions: ankle-wrist widening (from 11 to 0), stunting (from 6 to 4), underweight status (from 3 to 0), genu varum (from 6 to 2), and pot belly (from 2 to 0). However, genu valgum and frontal bossing showed little to no change.

  • Participants on the injectable stoss regimen also showed improvements: ankle-wrist widening (from 11 to 0), stunting (from 3 to 2), underweight status (from 1 to 0), genu varum (from 5 to 1), frontal bossing (from 1 to 0), and pathological fractures (from 2 to 0). Genu valgum and wind-swept deformity had minimal change.

  • Mild pain at the injection site was reported in approximately 80% (n = 16) of participants, while 1 patient experienced transient swelling and redness. The effects resolved spontaneously within 3 days. The pain or reaction did not interfere with overall compliance or the outcome of the treatment. No patients experienced severe pain or other local complications like abscess formation or nerve palsy.

Discussion

Nutritional rickets remains a significant public health issue, especially in developing countries, and has even re-emerged in some developed nations. Despite advances in healthcare, several factors continue to contribute to the high prevalence of rickets, including cultural practices, dietary restrictions, and environmental factors like air pollution and limited sunlight exposure [2]. Additionally, maternal vitamin D deficiency and increased fluoride content in groundwater have also been implicated in the persistence of this disease [7].

In our study, we aimed to compare the efficacy of two different vitamin D treatment regimens—oral weekly and injectable stoss therapy—in children diagnosed with nutritional rickets. The oral weekly regimen was based on the study by Singh et al. [8], which recommended 60 K IU/week for 10 weeks. The Stoss therapy consists of administering single dose of 6L IU intramuscularly [9].

Our findings showed that most participants were below 5 years of age, with no significant difference between the two treatment groups in terms of age. This aligns with previous studies from various regions, including the United States [10], Australia [11], and Nigeria [12], which reported a similar age range for the onset of rickets. Gender distribution in our study did not show any significant predisposition, consistent with other studies [13, 14], suggesting that rickets does not have a gender preference.

Vitamin D levels were similar at the study’s start and significantly increased in both treatment groups over time, with no significant difference between them at follow-up. Khan et al. [15] found higher levels in the injectable group, while K. Mondal et al. [16] reported no significant difference between groups at baseline or 12 weeks. Both groups showed a significant rise in serum 25(OH)D levels by 12 weeks, consistent with our findings. However, the injectable stoss therapy group showed a better-sustained increase in vitamin D levels at the 6-month follow-up. This could be attributed to the prolonged release of vitamin D from adipose tissue following a single large dose, which may enhance compliance by reducing the frequency of dosing.

Both treatment regimens resulted in significant improvements in serum calcium and phosphorus levels, consistent with the literature [16, 17]. Interestingly, while serum calcium levels were higher in the oral group at 6 months, serum phosphorus levels showed no significant difference between the groups throughout the study. The injectable stoss group initially had higher alkaline phosphatase levels, which decreased significantly over time, indicating ongoing bone healing. These biochemical changes suggest that both treatments are effective in correcting the mineral deficiencies associated with rickets, although the injectable regimen may offer more consistent long-term outcomes.

Recent studies have indicated that Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) levels are crucial markers in assessing Vitamin D deficiency and the effectiveness of treatment regimens. Our study builds upon this understanding by demonstrating significant differences in PTH level responses between injectable and oral Vitamin D3 therapies. Haffner et al. [18] explored the diagnostic workup in rickets and underscored the role of PTH in disease assessment. Dabas & Padidela [19] addressed the challenges in diagnosing and managing nutritional Vitamin D deficiency rickets in children, emphasizing the importance of PTH as a biomarker. In our study, we observed that the injectable regimen resulted in significantly higher PTH levels at the initial visit, possibly indicating more severe baseline disease. Both treatment regimens significantly reduced PTH levels from baseline, with notable decreases at 6 weeks and 3 months, and further reductions in the injectable group at 6 months. This suggests that PTH could serve as an early marker of both disease severity and treatment response, validating the reliability of PTH measurements in clinical practice. By confirming that PTH levels decreased significantly in both treatment groups, with most patients achieving normal levels by 6 weeks, our study adds new insights into the dynamic changes in PTH levels during different treatment regimens.

Thacher’s radiological score, used to assess the severity of rickets, showed significant improvement in both groups. By the end of the study, no child had a score indicative of active rickets, demonstrating the efficacy of both treatment regimens in achieving radiological healing. Clinical signs such as ankle and wrist widening, genu varum, and genu valgum were prevalent at the beginning of the study. While features like ankle and wrist widening resolved completely in all children, lower limb deformities such as genu valgum showed improvement but did not resolve entirely in most cases by 6 months. This highlights the need for early intervention and possibly longer follow-up to achieve complete resolution of skeletal deformities.

No children in our study reported symptoms of vitamin D toxicity or hypercalcemia. However, elevated vitamin D levels (> 100 ng/ml) were observed more frequently in the injectable stoss group, with occurrences at various follow-up points. Elevated serum calcium levels (> 10.6 mg/dl) were noted in a few children from both treatment groups, but none exhibited symptoms of hypercalcemia. These findings suggest that while elevated vitamin D and calcium levels can occur, they may be more common with injectable therapy, though typically without clinical symptoms.

Overall, our study confirms that both oral weekly vitamin D supplementation and injectable stoss therapy are effective in treating nutritional rickets. However, the injectable regimen may offer advantages in terms of sustained vitamin D levels and better compliance, making it a viable option for managing this condition, especially in settings where follow-up may be challenging. The findings also underscore the importance of early diagnosis and treatment to prevent long-term skeletal deformities and other complications associated with rickets.

Conclusion

This hospital-based study on Indian children (ages 6 months to 16 years) compared oral weekly vitamin D supplementation with injectable stoss therapy for treating nutritional rickets. We found no significant difference in the efficacy of the two regimens—both were equally effective and safe. However, injectable stoss therapy, being a single-dose, cost-effective option, may be more suitable for the Indian population, as it reduces the need for strict adherence compared to oral weekly therapy.

Serum parathyroid hormone (PTH) levels emerged as a useful early marker of rickets severity as well as treatment response. Biochemical parameters normalized within 6 weeks in both treatment groups, while radiological healing was achieved by 6 months. Persistent lower limb deformities often require surgical intervention, emphasizing the need for early detection and treatment.

Both treatment options are viable, with the choice depending on the patient’s and family’s ability to comply with treatment and follow-ups.

Limitations

The study’s scope could have been broadened through a multi-centre approach to include a larger and more diverse population. A larger sample size would also have provided more robust data. Additionally, while effective, the study did not extensively explore potential adverse effects such as hypercalcemia and hypervitaminosis D, which should be considered in future research.
