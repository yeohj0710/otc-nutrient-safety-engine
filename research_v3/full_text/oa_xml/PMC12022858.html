Key Points

Question

Is prophylactic receipt of intravenous (IV) magnesium associated with a lower incidence of cisplatin-associated acute kidney injury (CP-AKI)?

Findings

In this multicenter cohort study of 13 719 adults treated with a first dose of IV cisplatin between 2006 and 2022, receipt of IV magnesium was associated with a 20% lower adjusted odds of developing CP-AKI compared to those not receiving magnesium. These findings were supported by the results of multiple sensitivity and secondary analyses.

Meaning

These findings indicate that prophylactic IV magnesium administration was associated with reduced CP-AKI incidence, findings that warrant confirmation in randomized clinical trials.

Abstract

Importance

Cisplatin-associated acute kidney injury (CP-AKI) is a frequent complication of cisplatin chemotherapy and is associated with considerable morbidity and mortality. Prophylactic administration of intravenous (IV) magnesium attenuates CP-AKI in animal models; however, its association with CP-AKI in humans has not been rigorously evaluated.

Objective

To evaluate the association of prophylactic IV magnesium administration with CP-AKI in patients with cancer undergoing cisplatin chemotherapy.

Design, Settings, and Participants

This multicenter study was conducted at 5 major cancer centers across the US and included adult patients with cancer who were treated with a first dose of IV cisplatin between 2006 to 2022. Data analyses were performed from February to December 2024.

Exposure

IV magnesium vs no IV magnesium receipt on the first day of cisplatin treatment.

Main Outcomes and Measures

Composite outcome of CP-AKI or death, with CP-AKI defined as a 2-fold or greater increase in serum creatinine levels from baseline or receipt of kidney replacement therapy within 14 days after first dose of IV cisplatin. Secondary outcomes were CP-AKI or death, defined using alternative definitions, as well as major adverse kidney events at 90 days. Inverse probability treatment weighting was used to estimate the association between IV magnesium receipt and CP-AKI. Models were adjusted for demographics, comorbidities, laboratory values, receipt of concurrent nephrotoxic anticancer therapies, site, year of cisplatin administration, and cisplatin dose.

Results

A total of 13 719 patients were included (median [IQR] age, 59 [49-67] years; 7817 male [57%]), of whom 3893 (28.4%) received IV magnesium on the first day of cisplatin chemotherapy. The median (IQR) dose of IV magnesium was 2 (1-2) g. CP-AKI or death occurred in 104 of 3893 patients (2.7%) who received IV magnesium, and in 520 of 9826 (5.3%) who did not (adjusted odds ratio, 0.80; 95% CI, 0.66-0.97). Results were similar across a number of sensitivity analyses and secondary outcomes, including major adverse kidney events at 90 days.

Conclusions and Relevance

This multicenter cohort study found that patients with cancer who received prophylactic IV magnesium before initiating treatment with IV cisplatin had a lower risk of CP-AKI compared to those who did not receive magnesium. Randomized clinical trials are needed to confirm these findings.

This multicenter cohort study evaluates the association of prophylactic intravenous magnesium with cisplatin-associated acute kidney injury in patients treated with cisplatin chemotherapy.

Introduction

Cisplatin has been the cornerstone of treatment for a number of solid cancers for decades, and it remains first-line therapy for many malignant neoplasms today; however, cisplatin-associated acute kidney injury (CP-AKI) is a frequent and serious complication. CP-AKI increases susceptibility to extrarenal adverse effects from cisplatin and other renally-cleared chemotherapies, and also jeopardizes eligibility for future cisplatin and other renally-cleared anticancer drugs, especially when the kidney injury is irreversible.

Despite its clinical importance, no therapies have been shown to reliably reduce the incidence of CP-AKI. Data from adequately powered randomized clinical trials (RCTs) are lacking,1,2 and observational studies have had important limitations. For example, most studies on CP-AKI have been single-center and had small sample sizes, limited adjustment for confounders, and focused on milder degrees of kidney impairment that are less clinically relevant.3,4 Rigorously performed studies on CP-AKI prevention conducted with an appreciation for its underlying pathophysiologic mechanisms are therefore urgently needed.

Preclinical studies have found that prophylactic administration of intravenous (IV) magnesium attenuates CP-AKI, whereas magnesium deficiency increases susceptibility to it.5,6 These findings are explained by the effects of magnesium on the upregulation of drug efflux transporters on the apical membrane of kidney proximal tubular cells, facilitating enhanced urinary excretion of cisplatin and, therefore, less intracellular accumulation.7 However, the role of magnesium in attenuating CP-AKI has not been broadly studied in humans.

In the absence of adequately powered RCTs, data from observational studies may be used to guide clinical practice.8 In this study, we aimed to leverage data from 5 major cancer centers to evaluate the association of prophylactic IV magnesium with CP-AKI in patients with cancer initiating cisplatin chemotherapy.

Methods

The study was approved by the institutional review board at each participating site, and by the Mass General Brigham for the overall study. Consent was waived because the research involved no more than minimal risk to the participants, could not practicably be carried out without a waiver, and a waiver would not adversely affect the rights and welfare of participants. We followed the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) reporting guideline.

Data Source

We used data from a large multicenter cohort study of adults treated with a first dose of IV cisplatin between 2006 and 2022 at 5 major geographically diverse cancer centers across the US to evaluate the association between IV magnesium and CP-AKI or death (eTable 1 in Supplement 1).9

Study Population

The initial cohort comprised 31 082 adult patients with cancer initiating treatment with cisplatin. When selecting eligible patients for the analyses, we included and excluded patients based on whether they may or may not have been included in a randomized clinical trial. We included adults (≥18 years old) receiving their first dose of IV cisplatin between 2006 and 2022. We excluded patients with end-stage kidney disease (defined as receipt of long-term dialysis per International Classification of Diseases, Ninth or Tenth Revisions [ICD-9/10], at the time of cisplatin administration); missing data on baseline serum creatinine (SCr) or magnesium levels (baseline defined as the most proximal values in the 30 days before first dose of IV cisplatin); baseline serum magnesium level less than 1.4 or greater than 2.2 mg/dL (to convert to mmol/L, multiply by 0.4114); AKI at the time of cisplatin initiation (defined as a ≥2-fold SCr increase within 30 days) compared to baseline value (lowest SCr in the 365 days prior to cisplatin); and absence of at least 1 follow-up SCr value in the first 14 days after cisplatin initiation.

Outcomes

The primary outcome was moderate to severe CP-AKI or death. CP-AKI was defined as a 2-fold or greater rise in SCr or receipt of kidney replacement therapy (KRT) within 14 days after cisplatin receipt, consistent with modified stage 2 or 3 AKI (per Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes10) and with our prior study on CP-AKI.9 We focused on moderate to severe kidney injury because of its high-clinical relevance and used a 14-day window because CP-AKI routinely manifests within this time. We used inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW) with stabilized weights to estimate the association between receipt of IV magnesium on the day of IV cisplatin administration and the primary outcome of moderate to severe CP-AKI or death. Treatment assignment was based solely on receipt or no receipt of IV magnesium on the day of cisplatin treatment, irrespective of receipt of IV magnesium before or after that day.

Covariates

Covariates in the IPTW and multivariable models included age, sex, race and ethnicity, body mass index (BMI), comorbidities defined based on ICD 9/10 codes (diabetes, hypertension, congestive heart failure, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, cirrhosis, history of smoking), baseline estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) (calculated using baseline SCr and the 2021 equation from the Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration11), laboratory values (most proximal hemoglobin, magnesium, platelet count, serum albumin, white blood cell count) in the 30 days preceding receipt of IV cisplatin, receipt of concurrent nephrotoxic anticancer therapy (immune checkpoint inhibitors, pemetrexed, cetuximab, and ifosfamide) administered within 30 days before cisplatin treatment, site, year of cisplatin treatment (in 4-year bins), and cisplatin dose. Race and ethnicity, which were self-reported by participants, were assessed because data suggest there may be differences in CP-AKI based on these.12 Continuous variables were not categorized in multivariable models. Inclusion of variables in the final model was based on clinical knowledge, biologic plausibility, univariate associations, and parsimony. We used multiple imputation by chained equations to impute missing data, with 20 complete datasets created and results pooled using Rubin rules.

Secondary Outcomes

We also assessed several secondary outcomes, including CP-AKI or death occurring within a time frame of 10, 14, or 21 days, and using criteria for AKI that were either more liberal or more conservative compared to the primary outcome. For the more liberal criteria, we defined AKI as an increase in SCr greater than 0.3 mg/dL (to convert SCr to μmol/L, multiply by 88.4) or 1.5-fold or greater compared to baseline or receipt of KRT. For the more conservative criteria, we defined AKI as an increase in SCr 3-fold or greater from baseline or receipt of KRT. Finally, we examined major adverse kidney events within 90 days (MAKE90), defined as SCr 1.25-fold or greater than baseline at day 90, KRT within 90 days, or death within 90 days. Separately, we assessed MAKE90 in a similar manner but required a 1.5-fold or greater increase in SCr at day 90.

Statistical Analysis

E-Value Analysis

To assess the potential for unmeasured confounding, we conducted an E-value analysis using the primary model.13 The E-value quantifies the minimum strength of association that a hypothetical unmeasured confounder must have with both the exposure and the outcome, while simultaneously considering the measured covariates, to negate the observed exposure-outcome association. An advantage of the E-value is that, in contrast to other methods, it requires no subjective assumptions, such as the prevalence of the unmeasured confounder.13

Sensitivity and Subgroup Analyses

We performed several sensitivity analyses. First, we repeated the primary analysis using multivariable logistic regression without IPTW to adjust for confounding. Second, we expanded the exposure window for receipt of IV magnesium to include not only the day of cisplatin initiation but also the 3 days prior. Third, we limited the active treatment group to patients who received 2 g or more of IV magnesium. We also conducted subgroup analyses based on age (<65 vs ≥65 years), sex, baseline eGFR (<90 vs ≥90 mL/min/1.73m2), serum magnesium levels (1.4-1.9 vs 2.0-2.2 mg/dL), diabetes, and cisplatin dose (<50, 50-74, 75-99, and ≥100 mg). To assess for effect modification according to subgroup, we tested the significance of interaction terms (subgroup × treatment assignment) introduced into the model. Because of multiple comparisons, findings for subgroup analyses should be interpreted as exploratory.

Data analyses were performed from February to December 2024 using R, version 3.6.3 (R Foundation). All P values were 2-sided, with P < .05 considered statistically significant.

Results

A total of 13 719 patients (median [IQR] age, 59 [49-67] years; 5902 female [43%] and 7817 male [57%]; 786 Asian or Pacific Islander [5.7%], 1043 Black [7.6%], 1318 Hispanic [9.6%], 10 505 White [76.6%]; and 837 individuals of any other group or unknown [6.1%]) were included (Figure 1). The complete characteristics of the sample population compared with those excluded for missing data are shown in eTable 2 in Supplement 1. Among the 13 719, there were 3893 patients (28.4%) who received IV magnesium on the day of cisplatin initiation. The median (IQR) dose of IV magnesium was 2 (1-2) g. Baseline characteristics were largely similar between groups (Table), except that patients who received IV magnesium were less likely to have a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease or have a history of smoking. They also received a lower median (IQR) dose of cisplatin (67 [40-82] vs 75 [58-140] mg) than the patients who did not receive IV magnesium. Missing data for each variable are shown in the Table.

Among patients who did not receive IV magnesium, the composite outcome of CP-AKI or death within 14 days occurred in 520 patients (5.3%), of whom 460 (88.5%) developed CP-AKI without death, 43 (8.3%) died without CP-AKI, and 17 (3.3%) had both CP-AKI and died within 14 days. Among patients who received IV magnesium, 104 (2.7%) developed the composite outcome, of whom 86 (82.7%) developed CP-AKI without death, 10 (9.6%) died without CP-AKI, and 8 (7.7%) had both CP-AKI and died within 14 days. Patients who received IV magnesium had a lower odds of CP-AKI (adjusted odds ratio [OR], 0.80; 95% CI, 0.66-0.97) compared to patients who did not receive IV magnesium (Figure 2). The E-value for the primary model was 1.48, suggesting reasonable control of confounding.

Results were consistent across sensitivity analyses (Figure 2), including analyses using multivariable logistic regression without IPTW (Figure 2). There was effect modification based on age (<65 years: OR, 0.62 [95% CI, 0.45-0.84]; ≥65 years: OR, 0.94 [95% CI 0.73-1.21]; P = .04) and sex (male sex: OR, 1.11 [95% CI, 0.83-1.43]; female sex: OR, 0.64 [95% CI, 0.50-0.82]; P < .01), where the magnitude of benefit of IV magnesium was larger with younger age and with female sex (Figure 2). There was also effect modification based on eGFR (<90: OR, 0.89 [95% CI, 0.70-1.14]; ≥90: OR, 0.67 [95% CI, 0.52-0.87]; P = .05) and serum magnesium levels (1.4-1.9 mg/dL: OR, 0.99 [95% CI, 0.74-1.31]; 2.0-2.2 mg/dL: OR, 0.67 [95% CI, 0.52-0.87]; P = .04), where patients with higher eGFR and higher baseline serum magnesium levels appeared to benefit the most from IV magnesium. As shown in Figure 3, results were similar or greater in magnitude across nearly all secondary outcomes, including CP-AKI defined more liberally and more strictly. IV magnesium-treated patients also had a lower risk of MAKE90 compared to those not treated with IV magnesium.

Discussion

In this multicenter cohort study of more than 13 000 adults initiating treatment with IV cisplatin across 5 major US cancer centers, we found that those who received prophylactic treatment with IV magnesium had a lower risk of CP-AKI compared to those who did not. We rigorously adjusted for potential confounders, and our results were consistent across a number of sensitivity and secondary outcomes.

Our findings are consistent with preclinical findings, where magnesium deficiency increases susceptibility to CP-AKI and prophylactic administration of IV magnesium attenuates it.5 Magnesium deficiency downregulates efflux transporters expressed on the apical side of kidney proximal tubular cells (eg, multidrug resistance proteins 4 and 6),5,6 resulting in increased intracellular platinum accumulation. Administration of magnesium restores expression of these transporters, promoting the excretion of platinum in the urine. Furthermore, magnesium deficiency promotes immune cell−mediated inflammation in the kidneys, leading to increased production of inflammatory cytokines, such as interleukin-6, along with reactive oxygen species.14,15,16

Attempts to translate these preclinical findings to humans have been severely underpowered, with sample sizes ranging from 16 to 41 patients, thereby limiting conclusions regarding efficacy.17,18 Willox et al17 explored the effects of IV and oral magnesium supplementation in 16 patients receiving IV cisplatin for testicular cancer, 8 of whom were randomized to receive magnesium. AKI was more common in the nonsupplemented group. Importantly, there were no adverse events observed in 3 trials of pretreatment magnesium supplementation.17,18,19 In a pilot randomized clinical trial of patients with head and neck cancer that tested whether IV magnesium reduces the risk of AKI and acute kidney disease in patients receiving multiple cycles of cisplatin, the mean (SD) baseline serum magnesium levels were equivalent and in the normal range in both groups of patients (ie, 2.23 [0.14] mg/dL in those receiving normal saline [n = 15] and 2.18 [0.29] in those receiving IV magnesium [n = 15]).2 Patients receiving 16 mEq of magnesium added to normal saline had similar rates of CP-AKI (defined as an increase in SCr ≥0.3 mg/dL within 7 days; to convert SCr to μmol/L, multiply by 88.4) but lower rates of acute kidney disease (defined as an absolute increase in SCr ≥0.3 mg/dL from prechemotherapy to the SCr at completion of chemotherapy). These findings suggest that patients do not necessarily need to be hypomagnesemic at baseline to benefit from IV magnesium.

Strengths and Limitations

This study has several strengths, including use of data from a large number of geographically diverse patients initiating treatment with IV cisplatin across 5 major cancer centers, thereby enhancing the generalizability of these findings. Furthermore, we adjusted for a comprehensive set of potential confounders, and our findings were consistent across a number of sensitivity and secondary analyses. Finally, there was considerable interhospital variation in the use of IV magnesium (range, 5.5% to 85.0%). Inclusion of patients across a number of hospitals with different IV magnesium prescribing patterns allowed us to leverage this natural experiment to evaluate the association between IV magnesium and CP-AKI.

We also acknowledge several limitations. First, although we used IPTW to minimize confounding, there were important baseline differences among groups, particularly with regards to imbalances in cisplatin dose, and therefore, the possibility of residual confounding cannot be excluded. However, our E-value analysis suggested that a hypothetical unmeasured confounder would have to exert a large influence on our model to nullify the findings. Furthermore, we incorporated cisplatin dose in our IPTW model, and doses were similar between groups (absolute standardized difference, 0.01). Second, we did not have reliable data on home medications, such as oral magnesium and diuretics. However, the absence of adjustment for diuretics, which have been associated with hypomagnesemia and also potentially increase susceptibility to CP-AKI by causing volume depletion, would have only biased our results against IV magnesium. Third, we only collected data on the first cycle of IV cisplatin. Fourth, we could not reliably capture data on IV fluid administration before and after IV cisplatin due to the extended time frame of this study, use of multiple electronic medical records, and inconsistency in how IV fluid was documented. Fifth, we did not have reliable data on type or stage of tumors; however, these data would be unlikely to affect the study’s short-term outcomes (CP-AKI or death within 14 days).

Conclusions

The findings of this multicenter cohort study suggest that prophylactic administration of IV magnesium—a safe, inexpensive, and readily available intervention—was independently associated with a lower risk of CP-AKI in patients with cancer initiating cisplatin chemotherapy. RCTs are warranted to confirm these findings.
