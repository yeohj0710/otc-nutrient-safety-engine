Daily Versus Alternate Day Oral Iron Replacement for Women with Iron Deficiency Anaemia: A Randomized Controlled Trial

Abstract

The optimal way of oral iron replacement for iron deficiency anaemia(IDA) is still unknown and few recent studies have reported better fractional absorption and tolerability of intermittently administered iron. This randomised, active comparator controlled, open-label trial evaluated efficacy and tolerability of 120 mg elemental iron (2 × 200 mg ferrous sulphate tablets) administered as a single dose on alternate days (alternate-day arm, ADA) vis-à-vis 60 mg elemental iron (single FeSO4 tablet) administered daily (daily arm, DA), among women (age ≥ 18 years) having IDA, with haemoglobin (Hb) concentration between 6 and 10 g/dL and serum ferritin < 30 µg/L. Primary outcome was the Hb change on Day-28. Secondary outcomes included Hb change on Day-56, change in serum hepcidin levels from baseline to Day-14, adverse effects (AEs) and compliance to treatment. Sixty-eight participants (34 each in DA and ADA) were enrolled in the trial. Hb change at Day-28 was significantly higher in the ADA compared to DA (mean change: +2.2 ± 1.3 vs. +1.3 ± 1.1 g/dL, p = 0.003). Though Hb increment at Day-56 was also higher in ADA, it was not statistically significant. There was no significant difference in changes in serum hepcidin from baseline to Day-14 between the arms. Significantly higher proportion in the DA had AEs compared to ADA (45% vs. 9%, p < 0.005). However, treatment compliance was not significantly different between the arms. Alternate-day oral iron replacement led to significantly higher haemoglobin increment in the short run and caused significantly lesser AEs compared to daily replacement. Larger multicentric RCTs with longer duration of intervention should evaluate alternate-day replacement vis-à-vis daily therapy in future. This randomised controlled trial has been registered in Clinical Trial Registry-India (CTRI Reg. No.: CTRI/2021/12/038370, dated 2nd December 2021).

Keywords: Iron deficiency anaemia, Oral iron replacement, Alternate-day dosing, Serum hepcidin, Adverse effects, Treatment compliance

Introduction

Iron deficiency anaemia (IDA) constitutes a major public health problem in developing countries such as India [1]. IDA is more common among women and children. It has been estimated that 50–70% of Indian women in the reproductive age-group have anaemia [2] and iron deficiency is the predominant underlying cause. Oral iron treatment is the most commonly employed and physiological way of iron replacement for treatment of IDA. However, gastrointestinal adverse effects and inadequate enteral iron absorption may limit the utility of oral iron replacement. Adverse effects (AEs) resulting from oral iron treatment may reduce treatment compliance, thereby reducing its effectiveness. Thus, the optimal way of oral iron replacement to maximise the fractional absorption of ingested iron and at the same time limit its AEs is still unknown. Few recent studies suggest that alternate day oral iron replacement may be better tolerated [3, 4] and also lead to better iron absorption [4–7], compared to daily replacement. A study done with radiolabelled iron [5] in iron depleted women aged between 18 and 45 years and without anaemia has shown significant increase in serum hepcidin resulting from once daily or twice-daily oral administration of iron, which reduced fractional iron absorption from doses administered 12–24 h later. The same study found no significant hepcidin increase 48 h after the last dose and highest fractional iron absorption was noted at elemental iron doses between 40 and 80 mg.

Only one western study [7] has evaluated alternate-day vis-à-vis daily oral iron replacement in IDA. Rest of studies [5, 6] have evaluated the same in iron deficient non-anaemic women. Though two previous Indian studies [3, 4] have evaluated alternate-day versus daily iron replacement for treatment of IDA, the duration of oral iron replacement was significantly different between the daily and alternate-day replacement arms (to ensure equivalent cumulative doses of elemental iron in the two arms) in one study [3] and the other study [4] did not ensure equal cumulative doses of iron in the two arms during the three-week intervention period. With this background, this randomised controlled trial aimed to evaluate the efficacy and tolerability of alternate-day iron replacement vis-a-vis daily iron replacement for treatment of women with IDA, ensuring equal cumulative doses of iron administered in both the arms during the same length of intervention period.

Methods

This randomized, active comparator-controlled, open label, single centre clinical trial was conducted at a tertiary care teaching hospital located in southern India. Study participants were enrolled from December 2021 to July 2022. The study protocol was reviewed and approved by the Institute Ethics Committee for Interventional studies (Human studies) and written informed consent was obtained from all the trial participants before enrolment. This trial has been registered in Clinical Trial Registry- India (CTRI Reg. No.: CTRI/2021/12/038370).

Trial Participants and Eligibility Criteria

Women attending medicine outpatient services for anaemia were screened for eligibility to be included in this trial. Convenience sampling was followed for trial recruitment. Newly diagnosed women (age ≥ 18 years) with IDA, with Hb concentration between 6 and 10 g/dL, serum ferritin concentration of < 30 µg/L and peripheral blood picture consistent with IDA were eligible to be included in this trial. Pregnant and lactating women, those already diagnosed to have IDA and on oral iron therapy, those with known thalassaemia, malabsorption syndrome, peptic ulcer disease, coronary artery disease, cardiac failure, chronic kidney disease (serum creatinine ≥ 2 mg/dL), alcohol abuse (alcohol intake > once/ week), overt gastrointestinal bleed (hematemesis/ melena/ haematochezia) or receiving long-term (> 1 month) acid suppressive therapy and anaemia of chronic disease (anaemia seen in conditions such as systemic lupus erythematosus, rheumatoid arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, human immunodeficiency virus infection, tuberculosis etc.) were excluded from this trial. Those with dimorphic/ megaloblastic blood picture on peripheral blood smear were also excluded.

Randomisation and Study Intervention

Study participants were randomised with 1:1 allocation to receive either open label daily single dose 200 mg ferrous sulphate tablet (containing ≈ 60 mg of elemental iron) at 8 am (± 1 h) in empty stomach (daily replacement arm, DA) or 400 mg ferrous sulphate (2 × 200 mg ferrous sulphate tablets, containing together ≈ 120 mg of elemental iron administered as a single dose) at 8 am (± 1 h) in empty stomach on alternate days (alternate-day replacement arm, ADA). Generic ferrous sulphate (200 mg) tablets manufactured by Arya Pharmaceuticals, Indore, India were used in this trial. The day on which iron replacement started was considered as Day-0 in both the arms. The intervention was for a duration of eight weeks (from Day-0 to Day-55 in DA and Day-0 to Day-54 in ADA). Block randomization with variable block sizes was employed to generate randomization sequence. The generated sequence was protected by sequentially numbered opaque sealed envelope technique until study participants were recruited.

Outcome Assessment

Primary outcome assessed was the Hb change on Day-28. Secondary outcomes included Hb change on Day-56, change in serum hepcidin levels from Day-0 to Day-14, AEs related to oral iron therapy and compliance to treatment during first four weeks of replacement therapy in the two arms. Study participants were planned to be followed up on outpatient basis on Days-14, 28 and 56. In addition, participants were contacted by weekly telephonic calls to insist on compliance and to assess AEs related to oral iron replacement. Treatment related AEs were recorded as per the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE version 4.0) [8] during follow-up visits. Compliance to treatment was assessed based on pill counting (wherever feasible) or self-reporting by study participants during follow-up visits.

Laboratory Evaluation

Complete blood counts were done with Sysmex XT-2000i automated haematology analyser at enrolment and subsequently on Days-14, 28 and 56. Serum iron and unsaturated iron binding capacity (UIBC) were measured by spectrophotometry and serum ferritin by chemiluminescent assay, using Beckman Coulter™ analyser. Iron profile was done at enrolment and on Day-28. Serum hepcidin was measured by commercially available sandwich ELISA kit (Abbkine Scientific, China), at enrolment and on Day-14 (before intake of morning dose of ferrous sulphate) in both arms.

Statistical Analysis

Assuming a difference in Day-28 Hb increment of ∼0.7 g/dL between daily and alternate-day iron replacement arms and standard deviation of ∼1 g/dL [6], the sample size calculated was 64 (i.e., 32 participants in each arm), using nMaster software version 2.0 with 80% power and 5% alpha error. After assuming a loss to follow up of 5%, final sample was estimated to be 34 in each arm. Statistical analyses were done with IBM SPSS (Statistical Packages for Social Sciences) software version 19.0. Baseline characteristics of participants were presented by descriptive statistics and compared to assess the effectiveness of randomization. Categorical data was described using percentages and frequencies and compared by using chi-square test. Normally distributed continuous data between the two arms were compared by independent student t test and non-normal data by Mann-Whitney U-test. Per-protocol analysis was carried out to assess the outcomes, as there was loss to follow-up during the study.

Hb change at Day-28 was significantly higher in the ADA compared to DA (+ 2.2 ± 1.3 vs. +1.3 ± 1.1 g/dL, p = 0.003, Table 2). ADA showed significantly higher Hb change at Day-14 as well (Table 2). Hb increment of ≥ 2 g/dL at Day-28 was seen in 8 (25.8%) in DA and 15 (45.5%) in ADA. Though higher Hb increment was observed in ADA compared to DA at Day-56 as well (3.4 ± 1.7 vs. 2.2 ± 1.4 g/dL), the difference did not achieve statistical significance because of loss to follow-up of significant number of participants (Fig. 1, Table 2). There was significantly higher increase in reticulocyte count at Day-14 in the ADA compared to DA [median (IQR)) reticulocyte count (%) increase: 2.5 (2.4–2.9) vs.1.7 (1.3-2.0), p < 0.005]. Similarly, there were significantly higher changes in serum ferritin, serum iron and transferrin saturation from baseline to Day-28 in the ADA compared to DA (P < 0.05 for all, Table 3). There was no significant difference in changes in serum hepcidin from baseline to Day-14 in the DA compared to ADA [median (IQR) change (ng/mL): +0.3 (-4.95 to + 4.4) vs. -0.8 (-7.5 to + 2.9), p > 0.05].

More number of AEs were reported in the DA compared to ADA during first four weeks of follow-up (Table 4). Significantly higher proportion of participants in the DA had AEs compared to ADA (45% vs. 9%, p < 0.05). However, all reported AEs were of grade 1 severity (as per CTCAE v4.0 grading), except for one in ADA which was of grade 2 severity. Full (100%) compliance to treatment was observed in 28 (90.3%) participants in the DA and 32 (97%) in the ADA until Day-28. The rest 5 participants (3 in DA and 2 in ADA) had taken > 90% of doses of assigned treatment. Thus, treatment compliance was not statistically different between the two arms (p > 0.05, Fisher exact test). Among those 20 participants who could be followed up to Day-56 (Figs. 1), 100% treatment compliance was observed between Days-28 and 56.

Discussion

Intermittent dosing regimens of oral iron for treatment of IDA have evoked interest since the last decade. Cochrane systematic reviews have suggested that intermittently administered oral iron may be better tolerated and also be at least as effective as iron given daily for improving Hb in adolescent and adult menstruating women and also in pregnant women [9, 10].

In this open label randomised controlled trial (RCT), we compared head-to-head the Hb improvement with 200 mg ferrous sulphate tablet taken daily versus 400 mg of ferrous sulphate tablets taken on alternate days (administered as single dose) in women (age ≥ 18 years) having IDA and with haemoglobin concentration between 6 and 10 g/dL. Significantly higher Hb change was noted both at Days-14 and 28 in the ADA compared to DA. Though higher Hb change was noted in ADA even at Day-56, the difference did not achieve statistical significance because of significant attrition. These findings suggest higher efficacy of alternate-day replacement, at least in the short run. These results are consistent with a previous Indian study [4], which also reported higher Hb improvement at 21 days with alternate-day dosing of oral iron compared to daily dosing. Higher efficacy of alternate day replacement as compared to daily replacement may be explained based on higher fractional absorption of iron administered on alternate days, as reported in previous studies [6, 7]. However, another recently reported Indian randomised controlled trial [11] found no significant difference in Hb change at eight weeks between daily and alternate-day arms and also reported lower Hb increment in both arms compared to present study. The latter study included participants of both sexes with Hb < 10 g/dL and serum ferritin < 50 µg/L irrespective of bleeding source, as against the present study which excluded women with overt gastrointestinal source of blood loss. Further, the latter study enrolled more severely anaemic participants compared to the present one [mean baseline Hb in DA and ADA arms: 6.5 (± 1.89) and 6.7 (± 1.89) vs. 8.95 (± 0.93) and 8.2 (± 1.5) g/dL respectively] and did not elaborate on treatment adherence. Only about a fifth of women had history of menorrhagia in the present study and the latter study did not elaborate on the proportion of women who had menorrhagia. Thus, differences in the characteristics of enrolled participants and treatment adherence might account for differences in results observed in the two studies. The Hb increment in ADA in this study was comparable with that reported in another Indian trial [3].

No significant difference was observed between changes in serum hepcidin levels at Day-14 (from baseline) in the daily and alternate-day arms in this study, similar to observation in a previous Indian study at Day-7 [3]. However, higher hepcidin elevation has been reported with daily doses of oral iron during initial few days of treatment in other studies [4, 6]. Differences in the timing of assessment of changes in serum hepcidin concentration might account for these observed differences in the present and previous studies. Significantly higher improvements in serum ferritin, transferrin saturation (at Day-28) and reticulocyte counts (at Day-14) were observed in ADA compared to DA in this study, unlike few previous studies [11, 12]. Again, differences in the profile of enrolled participants may explain these discordant observations.

Significantly higher incidence of AEs related to oral iron treatment were observed in the DA compared to ADA in the present study, though all the observed AEs were of mild grade (grade 1 and 2 CTCAE v4.0) and the compliance to the treatment was not significantly different between the two arms. Previous studies have also reported favourable AE profile of alternate-day [3, 10, 12] and thrice weekly [13] iron compared to daily replacement. It should be noted that insistence through weekly telephonic calls might also have contributed to good treatment compliance rates in this study. Though AEs resulting from iron did not affect treatment compliance in this study, they may still lead to poor compliance and ultimately reduce the effectiveness of oral treatment in routine clinical practice, outside the clinical trial setting. Thus, in this regard, alternate-day iron replacement may offer advantages over daily therapy.

Strengths and Limitations

This study assessed a clinically relevant outcome such as Hb increment at three time-points (Days-14, 28 and 56) and also employed well tolerated doses of oral iron that are commonly used in clinical practice. The study design ensured equal cumulative doses of oral iron in both daily and alternate-day arms during the same length of intervention period, unlike some previous studies [3, 7]. Further, there was good adherence to treatment in both arms. However, this was an open label trial and absence of blinding might have affected the reporting of AEs. Because of the coronavirus disease-2019 (COVID-19) pandemic, there was significant attrition in this study beyond Day-28 (Fig. 1) and this affected evaluation of Hb increment at Day-56. Multiple serum hepcidin measurements, done at different time points during the intervention period, could have aided better understanding of serum hepcidin kinetics in the two arms. However, because of logistic and financial constraints, hepcidin could be measured at only one time point during the intervention period in this study.

Conclusion

Among women having IDA with Hb concentration between 6 and 10 g/dL, alternate-day oral iron replacement with 120 mg elemental iron led to significantly higher haemoglobin increment in the short run and caused significantly lesser AEs compared to daily replacement with 60 mg elemental iron. Larger multicentric RCTs with longer duration of intervention should be carried out in future to ascertain the benefit of alternate-day oral iron replacement vis-à-vis daily therapy.
